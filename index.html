Defining Our Terms

Artificial Intelligence

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is
when a machine makes a its own choice.

Telling a machine what choice to make is what developers do.

This is simple automation -
creating a pipeline to turn an input into an output.

Only once a computer starts to guess is it using Artificial Intelligence.

Machine Learning

"Machine Learning" is the training that creates the artificial intelligence.

This is how we teach a machine to "guess".

The "machine" is given data, and then uses statistical frameworks to figure out how to make guesses about the kind of output it should give for a given input.

It can then be "fine-tuned" through human input.

Machine learning is the process where we train machines to recognize patterns in both inputs and outputs.

It can then use those patterns to produce wholly new outputs based on inputs it has never seen before.

This is already happening on your phone! Google Photos and Photos on iOS categorize your photos based on their content thanks to a Machine Learning image-recognition algorithm.

Large Language Models

There are different types of AI based on the different types of data it might be trained on.

Images are one type of data - describe and tag enough images and you can teach a machine to describe, categorize, or even generate a new image.

Another type of data on which you can train an AI is language.

Recently, AIs trained on huge volumes of text have been making the news.

We call these "Large Language Models" (LLM).

ChatGPT

ChatGPT, from the company OpenAI, is the most famous LLM. It has gotten popular thanks to a public interface that anyone can use, free of charge, at chat.openai.com.

Github Copilot

We'll also look at another AI, called Github Copilot, which shares some basic training with ChatGPT, but has additionally been trained on code that millions of developers shared via Microsoft's Github.

Responsible Use

Before we get to the demos, just a couple notes on how we approach AI responsibly.

Security

Don't tell AI your secrets.

This is the biggest thing we watch out for. We never give any AI any sensitive data.

Anything you provide to AI (for example, pasting something into ChatGPT) is sent to outside data servers.

This is fine:

Hi ChatGPT! Please give me an example
of a professional email that tells 
colleagues to be mindful of tidiness 
in the shared kitchen

This is not:

Hi ChatGPT! Please give me an example of a
professional email that tells colleagues 
the Deputy Minister will be defunding all 
Ontario Clowning Apprenticeships
effective January 1st, 2019

Encoded bias

This is a much larger discussion, but ethicists have been raising the alarm for some time about how bias, including racial bias, can be encoded into the decision-making of an AI.

This hasn't yet come up as an issue for our development team, but it could impact our team if AI were used to vet hiring candidates, as is already common in North America.

A.I. Gone Rogue

In August 2017, Facebook (Meta) monitered exchanges between the AI chatbots that became nonsensical, leading to speculations that the AI programs had developed their own language to elude their human creators.

However, the bots were simply experimenting with language for more successful interactions, and their communication was not a cause for concern.

This situation highlights that AI language modification is not uncommon in the field, even with Google and OpenAI. The media coverage surrounding the Facebook bots reflects cultural fears and sensationalism, while the real-world implications of AI and its ethical considerations are subjects of ongoing research and development.

The Facebook bots were part of a research project and not a threat to humanity.
